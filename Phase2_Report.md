## 1. Introduction

Keystroke dynamics is a form of behavioral biometrics that identifies individuals based on how they type, rather than what they type. Every person has a unique typing rhythm — the way they press and release keys, the pauses between words, and the speed at which they transition between different letter combinations. These patterns are so distinctive that they can be used for user authentication, continuous identity verification, and even detecting whether text was typed by a human or generated by a machine (Alsultan & Warwick, 2013).

In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT have made it possible to generate text that is almost indistinguishable from human writing in terms of content and grammar. However, one area where LLM output still differs significantly from human behavior is in how the text is delivered. When a human types, the timing between keystrokes varies naturally — some letters come faster, some slower, with pauses at word boundaries and sentence endings. LLM output, on the other hand, is typically streamed at uniform intervals that lack this natural variability (Gonzalez & Calot, 2022).

This raises an interesting research question: **Can we design a function that takes LLM-generated text and streams it with realistic keystroke timing, making it indistinguishable from genuine human typing?** This question sits at the intersection of behavioral biometrics, machine learning, and human-computer interaction, and has practical implications for areas like chatbot design, accessibility tools, and security testing.

For this project, I am using the **KeyRecs dataset** (Dias et al., 2024), a publicly available keystroke dynamics dataset containing typing data from 99 participants. The dataset includes both free-text and fixed-text samples, recording inter-key latencies in a digraph format. My approach involves analyzing the statistical properties of real human typing, fitting probability distributions to the timing data, building machine learning models to distinguish human from synthetic typing, and then using those insights to build a simulation engine that replicates natural typing behavior.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Foundations of Keystroke Dynamics

Keystroke dynamics has been studied as a biometric modality since the early days of computing. The core idea is that typing patterns — specifically the timing of key press and release events — are unique to each individual. Killourhy and Maxion (2009) established one of the foundational benchmark studies in this field. They collected keystroke data from 51 participants typing a fixed password and evaluated 14 different anomaly-detection algorithms. Their work showed that distance-based methods like Scaled Manhattan distance could effectively distinguish between users based on their typing patterns alone. Their dataset from Carnegie Mellon University (CMU) became one of the most widely used benchmarks in keystroke dynamics research. What I found most useful from their work is how they structured the evaluation — comparing multiple detection algorithms on the same dataset with consistent metrics — which inspired the way I set up my own model comparison in this project.

### 2.2 Free-Text vs. Fixed-Text Keystroke Analysis

Most early research in keystroke dynamics focused on fixed-text scenarios, such as password typing, where the user types the same string repeatedly. However, free-text analysis is significantly more challenging and more relevant to real-world applications. Alsultan and Warwick (2013) conducted a comprehensive survey of free-text keystroke dynamics methods, highlighting the key differences between fixed and free-text approaches. In fixed-text, the same key sequences are repeated, so the analysis is relatively straightforward. In free-text, the text varies each time, so the system must generalize across different content. Their survey identified that digraph features (the timing relationship between pairs of consecutive keys) are the most commonly used features in free-text analysis, and that machine learning classifiers like Random Forest and SVM tend to perform well. This motivated my decision to focus heavily on bigram-level (digraph) analysis and to use ensemble classifiers in my project.

### 2.3 The KeyRecs Dataset

The dataset I am using for this project is the KeyRecs dataset, introduced by Dias, Vitorino, Maia, Sousa, and Praça (2024). KeyRecs contains keystroke data from 99 participants of various nationalities, who completed password retype and free-text transcription exercises. The dataset records approximately 1.6 million keystrokes and provides inter-key latencies computed in a digraph fashion — measuring the timing between each key press and release during an exercise. It also includes demographic information such as age, gender, and handedness. What makes KeyRecs particularly suitable for my research is that it provides both fixed-text and free-text samples, and the free-text portion has enough natural variability to serve as a ground truth for what "realistic" typing looks like. The authors designed the dataset specifically for training and testing machine learning models for anomaly detection and biometric authentication, which aligns well with my goal of training classifiers to detect synthetic typing.

### 2.4 Synthetic Keystroke Detection and Liveness

A line of research that is closely related to my project is the work on detecting synthetic (forged) keystroke sequences. Gonzalez and Calot (2022) published a study titled "Towards Liveness Detection in Keystroke Dynamics: Revealing Synthetic Forgeries," where they investigated whether naive implementations of keystroke verification can be fooled by synthetically generated timing data. They found that simple statistical models can generate keystroke sequences that bypass basic verification, but more sophisticated classifiers can still detect them. They also created a companion dataset of human-written and synthesized free-text keystroke samples specifically for evaluating liveness detection methods. This paper is the closest match to my own research question. The key difference is that while Gonzalez and Calot focused on detecting forgeries, I am approaching the problem from the opposite direction — I want to build a simulation engine that is good enough to actually fool trained classifiers. Their work gave me the benchmark to aim for and helped me understand what features classifiers rely on most when distinguishing real from synthetic data.

### 2.5 Deep Learning Approaches for Keystroke Biometrics

More recent work has explored the use of deep learning for keystroke dynamics. Acien, Morales, Monaco, Vera-Rodriguez, and Fierrez (2022) proposed TypeNet, a deep learning architecture specifically designed for keystroke biometrics. TypeNet uses recurrent neural networks to learn temporal patterns in typing sequences and achieved strong performance on both fixed-text and free-text authentication tasks. Their work demonstrated that deep learning models can capture subtle temporal dependencies in typing data that traditional feature-based methods might miss. While I chose to use ensemble methods (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting) rather than deep learning for my classifiers — primarily because my dataset size and scope made ensemble methods more practical — the insights from TypeNet about the importance of temporal sequence modeling influenced how I designed the windowed feature extraction in my pipeline.

### 2.6 Continuous Authentication and Typing Behavior Modeling

Mondal and Bours (2017) studied continuous authentication using keystroke and mouse biometrics. Their work is relevant to my project because they analyzed how typing behavior varies over extended sessions — including the effects of fatigue, typing speed changes, and rhythm drift. They found that user typing patterns are not perfectly static; they exhibit natural drift over time. This insight was important for my simulation engine, where I incorporated a fatigue drift mechanism that gradually modulates typing speed over long sequences. Without this kind of drift, simulated typing would appear unnaturally consistent, which is one of the signals that classifiers use to detect synthetic data.

### 2.7 Summary of Literature Findings

The literature review reveals several key insights that guided my approach:

1. **Digraph (bigram) features** are the most informative unit of analysis for free-text keystroke dynamics (Alsultan & Warwick, 2013; Dias et al., 2024).
2. **Per-bigram distribution parameters** capture more realistic variance than global statistics (Killourhy & Maxion, 2009).
3. **Synthetic keystroke detection** is achievable with high accuracy when synthetic data lacks natural variability (Gonzalez & Calot, 2022).
4. **Temporal dependencies and drift** must be modeled for realistic simulation (Mondal & Bours, 2017; Acien et al., 2022).
5. The **KeyRecs dataset** provides a solid foundation for this research with its large participant pool and detailed timing data (Dias et al., 2024).

## 3. Model Building

### 3.1 Overview

My approach consists of two major modeling components:

1. **Classification Models** — Machine learning classifiers trained to distinguish between real human keystrokes and synthetic (fake) keystroke data. These serve as the evaluation benchmark.
2. **Simulation Engine** — A function that takes any input text and generates realistic keystroke timing sequences by sampling from fitted probability distributions.

### 3.2 Data Preprocessing

I loaded the KeyRecs free-text dataset (`free-text.csv`), which contained 562,583 raw rows. After cleaning — dropping null values, removing corrupted entries, filtering extreme outliers (timing values > 10 seconds) — I was left with 559,485 clean rows across 99 participants. I also created derived features like key type classifications (alpha, space, punctuation, modifier) and contextual flags (word boundary, key overlap).

### 3.3 Feature Engineering

For the classification models, I computed windowed features over 20-keystroke windows. Each window was described by 19 features including:

- **DD flight time statistics**: mean, std, median, IQR, min, max, range, skewness
- **Hold time statistics**: mean, std, median
- **UD flight time statistics**: mean, std, negative ratio (overlap)
- **UU flight time statistics**: mean, std
- **Derived ratios**: hold-to-DD ratio, coefficient of variation for DD and hold times

This produced 26,430 human windows from the dataset.

### 3.4 Synthetic Data Generation

To train the classifiers, I generated three types of increasingly challenging synthetic data:

- **Statistical mimic**: Features sampled independently from per-feature distributions matching human mean and standard deviation, but without inter-feature correlations
- **Distribution-matched**: Each feature sampled from the empirical human distribution (column-independent bootstrap), preserving marginal distributions but breaking joint structure
- **Perturbed human**: Real human windows with 10–30% multiplicative noise, creating subtle distortions

### 3.5 Classification Models

I trained three ensemble classifiers:

- **Random Forest** (100 trees)
- **Gradient Boosting** (100 estimators)
- **AdaBoost** (100 estimators)

All models were trained on a 80/20 stratified split with StandardScaler preprocessing.

### 3.6 Simulation Engine

The simulation engine works by:

1. Looking up per-bigram distribution parameters (fitted during analysis) for each consecutive pair of characters
2. Sampling from the fitted Weibull or log-normal distribution to generate realistic DD flight times
3. Applying context-aware multipliers for word-start, mid-word, and word-end positions
4. Adding natural thinking pauses at sentence and paragraph boundaries
5. Incorporating fatigue drift that gradually modulates speed over time
6. Smoothing consecutive timings with momentum to create natural rhythm

### 3.7 Preliminary Results

The classification models achieved strong accuracy detecting realistic synthetic data:

| Model | Accuracy | F1 Score | AUC-ROC |
|---|---|---|---|
| Random Forest | 0.9816 | 0.9819 | 0.9990 |
| Gradient Boosting | 0.8911 | 0.9009 | 0.9753 |
| AdaBoost | 0.6644 | 0.7451 | 0.6959 |

When our simulation engine's output was evaluated against these same models, the results were mixed but promising. The AdaBoost model classified **82.6% of simulated windows as human**, and Random Forest classified **56.3% as human** — substantially above the near-0% rate for naive synthetic data. These results confirm that per-bigram distribution sampling effectively captures macro-level human typing patterns, although residual artifacts (particularly the absence of key overlap) are still detectable by stronger classifiers.
